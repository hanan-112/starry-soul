Vincent’s Masterpieces

A glimpse into the world through Van Gogh’s eyes.

Sorrowing Old Man

Painted in 1890 in Saint-Rémy de Provence completed in early May at a time when he was convalescing from a severe relapse in his health some two months before his death. In the 1970 catalogue raisonné, it was given the title Worn Out: At Eternity's Gate.

Starry Night

Painted in June 1889, it depicts the view from the east-facing window of his asylum room at Saint-Rémy-de-Provence,just before sunrise,

Almond Blossom

This is a group of several paintings made in 1888 and 1890 by Vincent van Gogh in Arles and Saint-Rémy. Flowering trees were special to Van Gogh.

Self-portrait

Painted in March-June 1887, Paris Vincent van Gogh painted about 35 self-portraits in total, most of them in Paris

Bedroom in Arles

The painting depicts Van Gogh's bedroom (first version) at 2, in Arles, France in 1888, known as the Yellow House.

Café Terrace at Night

Vincent painted this at night in Arles, France, in mid-September 1888. Not signed, but described and mentioned by him in three letters.

Starry Night Over the Rhône

The night sky and the effects of light at night provided the subject for some of Van Gogh's most famous paintings.

Vincent’s Messages

Quotes from Vincent's heartfelt letters...

“What would life be if we had no courage to attempt anything? Success is sometimes the outcome of a whole string of failures, and I tell you frankly that I often feel weak. But I press on nonetheless, even when I am tired and disheartened. The road is long, but it leads somewhere.” — To Theo, July 1880, Cuesmes, Belgium

“I often think that the night is more alive and more richly colored than the day. When I look at the stars, I imagine that perhaps we take death to go to a star, and we are there alive. Why, I ask myself, shouldn't the shining dots of the sky be as accessible as the black dots on the map of France? Just as we take a train to go to Tarascon or Rouen, we take death to go to a star.” — To Theo, September 1888, Arles

“I want to touch people with my art. I want them to say: he feels deeply, he feels tenderly. That’s what I hope to convey — not just colors or shapes, but emotion. When people look at my paintings, I want them to feel a human being stood behind the brush.” — To Theo, July 1889, Saint-Rémy-de-Provence

“I put my heart and my soul into my work, and I have lost my mind in the process. But what else is there for a man to do when the world is so full of injustice and indifference? Art is my reply, and painting is my way to breathe when I feel suffocated by life.” — To Theo, c. 1889, Saint-Rémy (at the asylum

“The way to know life is to love many things — animals, nature, people, even when they disappoint you. I have found more truth in a wheat field than in books, more meaning in a crow’s flight than in sermons. Love, and love again — and you will understand more than you know.” — To Theo, September 1880, Cuesmes

“I am seeking, I am striving, I am in it with all my heart. I do not work with ease. But I work with conviction, because something inside me will not be still. The brush is often heavy, my hand unsure, but I keep going. Because I believe art is not just seen — it is felt.” — To Theo, 1882, The Hague

“Let’s not forget that the little emotions are the great captains of our lives and we obey them without realizing it. A glance, a sigh, a passing sadness — these guide our steps more than grand ambitions. I’ve come to believe that tenderness, though quiet, has a stronger voice than pride.” To Theo, September 1889, Saint-Rémy

“There is peace even in the storm. At times I feel as though my mind is a whirlwind, but when I paint — a tree, a wheatfield, the sky — I find a calm that nothing else gives me. People may see madness, but I see motion and light and life. That is where my peace lies.” — To Theo, July 1889, Saint-Rémy-de-Provence

“It is looking at things for a long time that ripens you and gives you a deeper understanding. I often sit with a painting for hours — not painting, just watching. The lines begin to speak, the colors soften. Art, like life, is not to be rushed. One must dwell in it.” — To Theo, April 1885, Nuenen

About Vincent van Gogh’s Life

Vincent van Gogh was not just a painter . He was a soul that saw the world in bold, aching color. His art speaks louder than his words ever could.

Born in 1853 in the Netherlands, Vincent van Gogh created more than 2,000 artworks in just over a decade — a life driven by passion, color, poverty, and perseverance. Throughout his troubled journey, Van Gogh wrote more than 900 letters, many of them to his beloved brother Theo, in which he revealed his fears, philosophies, and unwavering love for art. These letters remain a window into his brilliant and aching mind. Despite his mental health struggles and a world that largely ignored his genius, he painted with relentless energy — capturing light, fields, people, and sky with a raw intensity. On 29 July 1890, after years of emotional suffering and isolation, Van Gogh died at the age of 37. Though he passed with little recognition, today his name echoes through the halls of every great museum, and his work continues to touch hearts across generations.
